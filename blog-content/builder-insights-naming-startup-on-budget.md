Naming agencies charge £10,000–£50,000 for a brand name. Some charge more. The process takes months. And the name you end up with is often something a founder with a good brief could have generated in an afternoon.

This isn't a knock on professional branding. It's a reminder that the expensive option isn't the only one — and at the early stage, it's rarely the right one.

## What You're Actually Paying for with a Naming Agency

Naming firms sell three things: process, expertise, and liability transfer. Their process involves stakeholder interviews, linguistic analysis, trademark clearance, and multiple rounds of refinement. Their expertise is real — experienced brand strategists have pattern-matched across hundreds of naming exercises.

The liability transfer is the underrated one. When a £30,000 name turns out to have trademark conflicts or cultural problems in a key market, it's the agency's problem, not yours.

For a funded company launching a flagship consumer brand, that value proposition makes sense. For a founder at the idea stage, naming an MVP, or building a bootstrapped SaaS — you're not buying a car. You're buying a bicycle. An agency fee at this stage is a misallocation.

The cost is free or near-free.

**Crowdsourced naming platforms** (Squadhelp, Naming Force) involve a brief submitted to a community of namers who compete to suggest options. You pay a prize amount (typically £150–£500) and get a large volume of suggestions. The quality is inconsistent — you'll get 200 suggestions and maybe 5 worth considering — but there's occasional genuine creativity that pure AI can miss.

The downside: turnaround is 5–10 days, there's still work in evaluating hundreds of options, and the winning name still needs trademark clearance on your own.

**DIY from scratch** — writing lists, working from mind maps, using thesauruses — works if you have good instincts and time. The risk is the founder attachment problem: you fall in love with a name because you made it, not because it's good.

## How to Evaluate Name Quality Without a Branding Expert

Agencies charge partly because name evaluation is genuinely hard. But the framework they use isn't proprietary. Here's a usable version:

**Phonetic test** — Can you say it naturally? Can someone spell it after hearing it once? Does it work across the accents of your target market?

**Clarity test** — Is there a plausible, positive interpretation? Does it evoke anything useful about what you do?

**Length test** — Ideally 6–10 characters. Under 15 at most.

**Uniqueness test** — Google it. Is there already a well-known brand using this name or something very similar?

**Trademark test** — Search the USPTO or UKIPO for existing marks in your product category. This is free and takes 10 minutes.

**The 48-hour test** — Do you still like it tomorrow? Show it to 5 people. What's their first association?

This is what a good naming agency does in a £30,000 engagement — except they interview more people, run linguistic analysis, and check trademark in 40+ territories. For a bootstrapped startup, the 80% version above is enough.

## Getting to a Shortlist Fast

The fastest path to a good name doesn't involve more options — it involves better filtering.

1. **Start with a brief.** Write down: what the product does, who it's for, 3 adjectives that should describe the brand, 3 that should definitely not.
2. **Generate broadly.** Run your core concept through an AI generator. Get 20–30 options.
3. **Eliminate, don't rank.** Cut anything that fails the phonetic test, trademark test, or availability check. You should be down to 5–8 options.
4. **Score the survivors.** Apply the quality framework above. Use a scoring tool if available.
5. **Sleep on your top 2.** The name that still feels right after 48 hours is your name.

The whole process takes a day, not months. And you won't need to spend £30,000 to do it.
