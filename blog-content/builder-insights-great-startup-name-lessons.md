# What Makes a Great Startup Name? Lessons from Stripe, Notion, and Figma

**Category:** Builder Insights · ⏱ 8 min read

**Target keyword:** what makes a good startup name
**Secondary keywords:** startup naming tips, best startup names, how to name a startup

---

Some startup names feel inevitable in retrospect. Stripe sounds like exactly what a payment company should be called. Notion fits a thinking tool perfectly. Figma is the kind of name that sounds like it couldn't have been anything else.

But these names weren't discovered — they were built. Each one satisfies a set of criteria that most founders don't consciously apply when they're naming their own companies. Let's decode them.

## Stripe: One Syllable, Clean, Evocative

Stripe is four letters, one syllable, and carries a subtle implication of streamlining. It doesn't mean "payment processing" — it suggests simplicity, a clean line, something elegant running through complexity.

What makes it work:
- **Length:** Four characters. Types instantly, says instantly.
- **Phonetics:** Hard consonant start (St-), clean vowel, crisp ending. Easy across accents and languages.
- **Brandable neutrality:** It doesn't mean payments, so Stripe could expand into banking, financial infrastructure, or anything adjacent without the name becoming a liability.
- **Domain:** Stripe.com. Clean. No modifiers.

The lesson from Stripe isn't to copy the style — it's to understand that the best names suggest rather than describe. They evoke a feeling or quality rather than naming a feature.

## Notion: Abstract But Memorable

Notion is a real English word meaning a concept or idea. For a productivity tool designed around thinking, it's semantically apt. But the genius is that "notion" is just abstract enough that it doesn't lock the product into a single use case.

You can have a Notion for notes. For projects. For wikis. For databases. The name works because it's a container for whatever you need it to be.

This category of name — a real word, loosely evocative, distinctly not generic — is arguably the hardest to find because the obvious ones are taken. But "Notion" worked because:
- It was available on .so at launch (later moved to Notion.so, later acquired the .com)
- The word wasn't already associated with any major tech product
- It's one syllable when spoken casually ("I use Notion for everything")

The lesson: a real word can work as a brand name if it's not already crowded and it carries the right emotional valence.

## Figma: Invented, Phonetically Strong

Figma is a made-up word. It may derive from the Latin "figma" (meaning form, shape) — appropriate for a design tool — but in practice, nobody knew that when they first heard it. It worked anyway.

Made-up words are the highest-risk, highest-reward naming strategy. When they work, they're perfect: no existing associations, no trademark conflicts, completely ownable. When they fail, they're just noise.

Figma succeeds because:
- **It sounds like a word.** Two syllables, easy vowel-consonant pattern. Your brain can parse it.
- **It has a strong start.** "Fig-" is distinctive. The hard 'g' sound lands.
- **It's not trying too hard.** Many invented words fail because they're obviously constructed — they sound like a brand exercise. Figma sounds like it could be a word you've just forgotten.

The lesson: if you're going to invent a word, it needs to sound like a real one.

## The 5 Traits of Names That Scale

Looking across Stripe, Notion, Figma — and adding Slack, Zoom, Shopify, Linear, Vercel — consistent patterns emerge:

1. **Short.** All are under 10 characters. Most are under 7. Length is the #1 operational liability in a name.
2. **Pronounceable on first encounter.** Native speakers of different languages can attempt them. International teams can say them in meetings without embarrassment.
3. **Invented or not-yet-associated.** Either completely made up or a real word with no dominant tech association already attached.
4. **Domain available.** Not always .com at launch, but they eventually controlled the primary TLD.
5. **No negative connotations in target markets.** This requires checking — Mitsubishi's "Pajero" famously fails this test in Spanish-speaking markets.

## Mistakes That Kill Early-Stage Brands

**Being too literal.** If your name describes exactly what you do today, it will limit you the moment you expand. A company called "InvoiceTracker" has a harder pivot to general financial tooling than "Wave" does.

**Being too generic.** Single adjectives (Smart, Simple, Clear) create brand search nightmares. You'll spend your entire marketing budget competing with every other context that word appears in.

**Chasing availability over quality.** The fact that a name is available on .com is not a reason to use it. Many terrible names are available because nobody wanted them.

**Ignoring the spoken test.** Founders evaluate names by looking at them. But your name will be spoken in meetings, on calls, in pitch rooms, and on podcasts. Say it out loud twenty times. Does it still feel right?

**The Founder Attachment problem.** You come up with a name you love and stop evaluating objectively. The antidote: share it with five people who have no investment in your success and ask for their genuine first impressions.

💡 **The five-years-from-now test:** Imagine your company has grown 10x. You're announcing a major product expansion into a new category. Does your name still work? If it feels limiting, you're building a naming problem into your foundation.

## Building the Name You Don't Have to Change

The ideal startup name is one you can grow into, not grow out of. It should feel slightly larger than your current product — a container with room to fill.

Stripe was doing payments when they launched. Now they're building financial infrastructure for the internet. The name still works.

Notion was a note-taking app. Now it's a company OS. The name still works.

Choose a name with that kind of generosity, and you'll never have to spend six figures on a rebrand.
