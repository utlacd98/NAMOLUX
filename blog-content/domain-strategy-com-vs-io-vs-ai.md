# .com vs .io vs .ai: Which Domain Extension Should Your Startup Use in 2026?

**Category:** Domain Strategy · ⏱ 7 min read

**Target keyword:** best domain extension for startups
**Secondary keywords:** .com vs .io, .ai domain, startup domain extension

> ⚠️ NOTE: This article is already covered on the blog under slug `best-domain-extensions-2026`. Review that post before publishing this version to avoid duplicate content.

---

Domain extensions used to be a simple choice. .com for everyone. Now there are dozens of viable options, and founders are genuinely torn between them. The stakes are real — your extension affects trust, cost, SEO perception, and how seriously investors take you.

Here's a practical framework for making this decision once, correctly.

## The .com Case: Still the Default for Good Reason

.com owns 48% of all registered domains globally. That number matters because it reflects what users trust. When someone hears your company name, their first instinct is to type it with .com. If that takes them somewhere else, you've handed traffic to another site.

The trust advantage of .com is particularly strong with non-technical audiences. Enterprise buyers, older demographics, and press outlets all default to .com assumptions. For a B2B SaaS targeting Fortune 500 companies, .io might raise an eyebrow. For a dev tool targeting engineers, nobody cares.

**When .com is non-negotiable:**
- You're targeting mainstream consumers
- You're fundraising from traditional VCs
- You're building a brand meant to last 10+ years
- You want to sell the company eventually (acquirers prefer .com)

## The .io Case: Earned Its Place in Tech

.io was originally the country code for the British Indian Ocean Territory. Nobody cares about that anymore. It became the de facto standard for developer tools, SaaS products, and anything tech-adjacent, partly because so many .com names are taken and .io still has inventory.

The perception of .io has shifted. Stripe, GitHub, Notion — these companies have trained people to expect .io from credible tech startups. If your .com is unavailable or expensive (£1,000+), .io is a legitimate alternative.

The downside: .io costs roughly 3–5x more than .com (typically £35–£60/year vs £10–£12/year), and the pronunciation ambiguity ("eye-oh" vs "ee-oh") occasionally causes issues in verbal contexts.

**When .io works:**
- Developer tools, APIs, SaaS platforms
- Target audience is technical
- The .com version is taken or priced out of reach
- You're comfortable paying the premium

## The .ai Case: Powerful Signal, Narrowing Fast

.ai is technically Anguilla's country code domain, but nobody treats it that way. In 2023–2026, .ai became the clearest signal a company name can send: this product uses artificial intelligence.

That clarity is both an advantage and a trap. If AI is genuinely core to what you do, .ai reinforces your positioning every time someone reads your URL. If you're using .ai because it sounds cool while running mostly conventional software, you're making an implicit promise you can't keep.

.ai domains have become expensive. Premium .ai registrations often start at £60–£90/year. And the space is getting crowded — many good .ai names are already taken or priced in the thousands on the aftermarket.

**When .ai makes sense:**
- AI is genuinely central to your product
- You're targeting a technical, AI-literate audience
- You have a short, distinctive brand name that works well with .ai
- You're prepared to own the "AI company" positioning

## What About .co, .dev, .app, and Others?

**.co** — Used by some well-known brands (Typeform.com vs Typeform.co). Gets confused with .com constantly. Use it only if you're confident your audience is online-savvy.

**.dev** — Google's TLD, reserved for developers. Good signal for developer tools, but limited to that audience. Requires HTTPS by default, which is a feature not a bug.

**.app** — Works for mobile apps. Descriptive but narrow. Same HTTPS requirement as .dev.

The rest (.xyz, .online, .site, .club) tend to reduce credibility more than they save in cost. The trust deficit is usually not worth the savings.

## Does Domain Extension Affect SEO?

Directly: no. Google has confirmed that TLDs are not a ranking factor. A well-built site on .io will outrank a poorly-built site on .com.

Indirectly: yes, in two ways. First, .com domains tend to get more organic direct traffic because users assume it. Second, trust signals from the extension affect click-through rates in search results — CTR is a ranking signal.

💡 **Pro tip:** If you can't afford the .com and choose .io or .ai, buy the matching .com anyway (even if someone else owns it, register the nearest available variant). Prevent brand confusion before it starts.

## A Framework for Deciding

Run through this in order:

1. Is the .com available for under £500? **Buy it.**
2. Is your audience primarily technical? **.io is viable.**
3. Is AI genuinely core to your product? **.ai adds positioning value.**
4. Are you targeting mainstream consumers or investors? **Keep searching for the .com.**
5. Everything else? **Keep searching.**

The extension is a detail, not a strategy. A memorable, brandable name on a second-tier extension will outperform a forgettable name on .com. But all else being equal, .com wins.
