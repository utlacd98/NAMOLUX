# How to Choose a Domain Name That Actually Builds Your Brand

**Target keyword:** how to choose a domain name
**Secondary keywords:** domain name tips, brandable domain name, domain name strategy

Most domain name advice focuses on availability and cost. That's not wrong, but it misses the point. The real question isn't "can I register this domain?" — it's "will this name help me build a business?"

The best domain names do something technical tools can't measure: they stick in people's heads, travel well through word of mouth, and make your brand feel inevitable in retrospect.

## The Radio Test

This is the single most useful filter for a domain name. Imagine someone heard your company name on a podcast or a radio ad. Could they spell it correctly on their first attempt?

Fail conditions:
- Unusual spellings (Lyft, Fiverr, Tumblr)
- Homophones that might be spelled multiple ways ("ware" vs "wear" vs "where")
- Silent letters or non-obvious pronunciation
- Multiple words run together where the split is ambiguous (expertsexchange.com, famous for the wrong reasons)

If you need a tagline to explain how to spell your name ("it's spelled with two p's"), the name is working against you.

## Short vs Clever: Pick One

Founders love clever names. A pun, a portmanteau, a reference to something cultural. Clever names can work brilliantly — Mailchimp, Slack, Figma are all slightly unexpected choices that became iconic.

The problem is that most clever names are only clever to the person who invented them. What feels like a sophisticated cultural reference in week one becomes an unexplainable choice by year three.

Short is a more reliable target than clever. Aim for 6–10 characters. Anything over 15 is a liability for direct traffic and word of mouth. Under 6, and you'll struggle with trademark conflicts and available domains.

## Brandable vs Descriptive: A Key Distinction

A **descriptive name** tells you exactly what the business does. BestAccountingSoftware.com is descriptive. These names have short-term SEO advantages and immediate clarity.

A **brandable name** could theoretically mean anything but becomes associated with your business specifically. Google doesn't describe a search engine. Stripe doesn't describe payment processing. Notion doesn't describe a productivity tool.

Brandable names scale better because they don't lock you into one category. When you pivot, expand, or get acquired, a brandable name travels with you. A descriptive name becomes a cage.

💡 **The test:** Can your name appear as a standalone noun in a sentence and make sense? "Just send it over Slack." "It's saved in Notion." Names that become verbs or places win the branding long game.

## The 5-Point Checklist Before You Register

Before you commit to any domain, verify all five of these:

1. **Say it aloud three times.** Does it feel natural? Would it embarrass you to say it in a client meeting?
2. **The Radio Test.** Can someone spell it from hearing it? (See above)
3. **Trademark check.** Search the USPTO (US) or UKIPO (UK) for existing marks in your category. This is the most overlooked step.
4. **Google it.** Does anything negative appear? Is there an existing company with the same or similar name?
5. **Social handle availability.** Check Twitter/X, Instagram, LinkedIn. You don't need every platform, but you need the ones your customers use.

## The Overnight Test

Sleep on it. Seriously.

Names feel different at 10am on a Tuesday than they do after a long brainstorm session at midnight. If you still like it the next morning, it passes. If you've started to see the flaws, that's important information.

Then give it 48 hours before you register. Send it to five people who aren't invested in your success and ask them what the name makes them think of. Their first impressions are a proxy for your customers' first impressions.

## What to Do When You're Stuck

If every good name you think of has a taken .com, you have three options:

1. **Add a qualifier** — GetMailer, UseNotion, TryFigma. Works at launch, ideally you drop the qualifier as you grow.
2. **Try a different TLD** — .io, .ai, or .co depending on your audience.
3. **Let an AI generate alternatives** — give it your core concept and iterate. The name you end up with is often not your first idea, and rarely your tenth.

The goal is to find something that feels right, passes the five-point check, and doesn't limit where you can take the business.
