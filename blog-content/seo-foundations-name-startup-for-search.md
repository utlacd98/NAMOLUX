# How to Name Your Startup for Search: SEO-Friendly Naming Strategy

**Category:** SEO Foundations · ⏱ 7 min read

**Target keyword:** SEO-friendly business name
**Secondary keywords:** startup name SEO, how to name a startup, brandable name vs keyword name

---

Most founders name their startup based on what sounds good in a pitch deck. Few think about what happens when someone Googles it two years later. The name you choose now will either compound your search authority or fight against it for the life of the business.

Here's how to think about naming from an SEO perspective — without sacrificing the brand.

## Why Brandable Names Beat Keyword Names Long-Term

The intuition behind keyword names makes sense: if you name your company "Fast Accounting Software", people searching "fast accounting software" might find you. This works briefly, then creates persistent problems.

**The dilution problem:** Generic keyword names compete with editorial content, forum posts, comparison articles, and dozens of other sites that also rank for those terms. You don't own the phrase. You're just one of many entities that happen to use it.

**The pivot problem:** When you expand, change focus, or get acquired, a descriptive keyword name becomes misleading. Stripe could expand into banking infrastructure. "PaymentSimplified.com" is a trap.

**The search volume problem:** Branded search queries — people searching specifically for your company — are the highest-intent traffic you'll ever get. A brandable name builds a proprietary search category. Nobody else can rank for "Notion" except Notion.

HubSpot, Mailchimp, and Intercom are all brandable names with no obvious keyword meaning. Each of them now dominates branded search in their category — not because of keywords in their names, but because their names are distinctive enough that brand searches compound over time.

## The Searchability Problem: Names That Compete with Themselves

There's a trap on the other end too. If you choose a name that's a common English word, you'll spend years fighting for search visibility against every other use of that word.

Simple.com (banking app) had to compete with millions of pages that just use the word "simple". Asana (project management) shares its name with a yoga pose. These companies built strong enough brands to overcome it, but it required significantly more investment in content and link building than if they'd chosen more distinctive names.

Test this before you commit: Google your proposed name. If the first two pages are full of unrelated content using that word, you have an uphill battle for brand name search visibility.

**Names most likely to have this problem:**
- Single common English words (Simple, Plain, Clear, Pure)
- Everyday verbs (Flow, Build, Make, Launch)
- Adjectives used across industries (Smart, Fast, Easy, Pro)

**Names that avoid it:**
- Invented words (Figma, Zara, Spotify)
- Unusual combinations (Mailchimp, HubSpot, Salesforce)
- Names with distinct phonetic signatures (Stripe, Slack, Zoom)

## How Brand Search Volume Builds Authority

When someone types your company name into Google, that's a branded search. When thousands of people do it, Google interprets it as a signal that your brand has real-world relevance.

This is a compounding effect. More branded searches → higher perceived authority → better rankings for non-branded terms → more site visitors → more people who know the brand → more branded searches.

💡 **The flywheel in practice:** A startup with a distinctive name that earns 500 branded searches/month in year one will naturally build domain authority faster than a keyword-named competitor with 50 branded searches/month — even if content quality is identical.

You can accelerate this with PR, social media, and word-of-mouth. But the foundation is a name people actually remember and specifically search for.

## How to Test Searchability Before You Commit

Run your top 3 name candidates through these five checks:

1. **Direct Google search** — Search the exact name. Are results about your proposed company or something else entirely? How many results total?

2. **Google Trends** — Compare your candidates. Flat-line means nobody searches it (good for a new brand, means you'll own it). Spiky patterns mean existing associations you'll fight.

3. **"[Name] + your category" search** — Search "[name] software" or "[name] app". If nothing comes up, you have clean territory.

4. **Social handle check** — Search the name across Twitter/X, Instagram, LinkedIn. The username availability tells you how saturated the name already is.

5. **The news search test** — Search the name in Google News. Existing media coverage means an established entity using that name. That could create confusion or citation conflicts.

None of these tests takes more than 10 minutes combined. Doing them before you register could save you years of confusion.

## Avoiding Names That Compete with Common Words

The practical rule: if your brand name could finish the sentence "the [name] of the [object] is unclear", you have a problem.

Test your name in isolation. Put it in a Google search with no other context. What comes up? Those results are the pre-existing associations you're fighting against.

For SEO purposes, the ideal name:
- Returns zero or near-zero results when searched alone
- Has no major brand or cultural entity already using it
- Can be combined with your category keyword to create distinctive search phrases ("Figma tutorial", "Stripe API", "Notion template")

The name you choose creates the search landscape you'll operate in for the next decade. Choose one that gives you room to build, not one you'll have to fight through.
